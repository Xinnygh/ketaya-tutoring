A-level Maths from C to A*: What Actually Works (2026)

By DAYA Elite Tutoring · International curricula & admissions tutors · Updated 2026

Many students plateau at a B or C in A-level Maths, and parents assume it's a lack of talent. In fact A-level Maths is one of the most method-driven, most improvable subjects — it rewards trained fluency and accurate execution, not flashes of genius. This guide explains why students get stuck, the three things that actually move the grade, and a revision routine you can follow.

In this guide
  • Why students plateau (usually not because it's hard)
  • The three things that actually move the grade
  • A revision routine that works
  • What a tutor accelerates

1. Why students plateau at a B or C

The reason students get stuck is rarely "the topics are too hard." It's a handful of silent errors quietly costing marks again and again —

  • Shaky algebra fundamentals: if expanding brackets, fractions, indices and logs are slow or error-prone, the whole question collapses after them.
  • Not showing working, losing method marks: A-level Maths awards marks step by step — skipping steps means throwing away method marks.
  • Misreading command words: "show that", "hence", "in exact form" each demand something specific — miss one and you lose marks (especially a risk for Chinese students reading English stems).
  • Revising the wrong way: re-reading notes and watching worked solutions, but rarely doing full past papers under time pressure — the most common kind of fake effort.

2. The three things that actually move the grade

① Make algebra fast and error-free

The biggest gap between an A* and a C student is often not the hard questions but the speed and accuracy of basic manipulation. Once the basics are solid, there's time and headspace left for the hard parts. Spend a week or two on this first — it has the highest return.

② Drill by question type, not just by chapter

Exams mix topics together. Practising only chapter by chapter leaves students lost on synoptic questions. The fix: train by the question types that appear in your board's past papers, learning to recognise "which type is this, which method applies" until it becomes automatic.

③ Review mistakes — don't just move on

For every wrong answer, ask: was it a concept gap, a calculation slip, or a misread? Keep an error log, sort mistakes by cause, and redo them regularly. Most of the grade gain comes from permanently eliminating the same recurring mistake.

3. A revision routine that works

PhaseWhat to doGoal
Weeks 1-2Fix algebra basics + find weak topicsFast, accurate basics
Weeks 3-6Drill past papers by type, build error logRecognise types, kill repeat errors
Final 2-3 weeksFull timed mocks + redo the error logSpeed, accuracy, exam pacing
One rule: do full papers, timed, like the real thing — then mark your own work against the mark scheme. This step exposes exactly where marks are lost, and it's the step most students skip.

4. What a tutor accelerates

Self-study works, but a tutor removes a lot of trial and error: quickly pinpointing exactly where you're stuck, fixing it by the shortest route, correcting mark-losing habits you can't see yourself, and squeezing out every mark against the mark scheme. For Chinese students, bilingual teaching also explains hard parts in Chinese while making sure the English answer wording scores.
