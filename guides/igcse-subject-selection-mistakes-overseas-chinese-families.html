IGCSE Subject Selection: 5 Mistakes Overseas Chinese Families Make (2026)

By DAYA Elite Tutoring · International curricula & admissions tutors · Updated 2026

Choosing IGCSE subjects looks like just ticking a few boxes — but it's the first foundation for your child's A-levels and university applications. Get it wrong and you only find out two years later, when fixing it is costly. Here are the 5 mistakes overseas Chinese families make most, plus a simple way to choose well.

  • Mistake 2: ignoring the IGCSE-to-A-level bridge (especially Maths)

  • Mistake 5: treating English as a 'later' problem

Mistake 1: Choosing for today's strengths, not the university goal

Many families pick whatever subject the child currently scores highest in. The problem: many top UK/US courses have hard A-level subject requirements, and those A-levels rely on specific IGCSE foundations. Choose by working backwards from the university direction your child is aiming at — not just from today's grades.

Example: Medicine usually needs A-level Chemistry + Biology (often Maths too); Engineering / Computer Science almost always needs Maths and strongly favours Physics. The foundation for all of these must be laid at IGCSE.

Mistake 2: Ignoring the IGCSE-to-A-level bridge (especially Maths)

The classic trap: a child who plans to take A-level Maths / Further Maths or Physics, but didn't take IGCSE Additional Maths. A-level then feels brutal from day one because a big bridging chunk is missing. For science-track students, the IGCSE maths foundation must be solid.

Mistake 3: Piling on too many subjects

"More subjects is safer" is a common myth. Universities and A-level entry typically look for 5-8 strong grades (including English and Maths), not sheer quantity. Better to get A/A* in 7-8 subjects than a pile of B/C across 10. Quality > quantity.

Mistake 4: Letting an easy subject crowd out the core ones

Taking IGCSE Chinese (First Language) is usually smart for Chinese students — an easy high grade that lifts the overall profile. But it should be a bonus, not a substitute: don't let an easy grade replace the core academic subjects your child's target course actually requires.

Mistake 5: Treating English as a 'later' problem

Many Chinese students are strong in science, so parents set English aside. But IGCSE English is a near-universal hard requirement for universities and A-level — and science exams are set entirely in English too. If your child can't read the question or write the terminology, strong science still loses marks. English is foundation, not an elective.

This is exactly where bilingual teaching helps: explain the hard science in Chinese while drilling the English exam wording — neither side is neglected. See our online tutoring guide.

A simple way to choose

  1. Start with direction: roughly what field / country is your child aiming at (medicine, engineering, business, computing…)?
  2. Lock the core: secure English and Maths; then add Additional Maths / Physics / Chemistry / Biology / Economics by direction.
  3. Add a booster: use a high-confidence subject like IGCSE Chinese to steady the overall profile.
  4. Cap the count: 7-9 subjects, each one you can realistically push to A/A*.
  5. When unsure, ask: send the report card and the goal to a tutor who knows the syllabus and let a professional work it backwards.
